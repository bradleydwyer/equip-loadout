MAJORITY OPINION
───────────────────────────────────────────
Inflation is a sustained rise in the overall price level (so purchasing power falls). It typically results from a combination of forces:

1) Demand‑pull inflation: Aggregate demand (consumer spending, business investment, government spending, exports) rises faster than the economy’s capacity to produce, so prices get bid up. Often associated with strong growth, fiscal stimulus, easy credit/low interest rates.

2) Cost‑push inflation: Production costs increase (energy, raw materials, shipping, tariffs, wages not matched by productivity, supply-chain disruptions). Firms pass higher costs into final prices, especially when supply is constrained.

3) Expectations / “built‑in” inflation: If households and firms expect higher inflation, workers seek higher wages and firms raise prices preemptively, potentially creating a wage–price spiral that makes inflation more persistent.

4) Monetary/credit conditions: Over the medium to long run, inflation is closely tied to money and credit growth relative to real output. If money/credit expansion persistently outpaces productive capacity, spending tends to exceed supply and prices rise.

5) Other amplifiers and shocks: Exchange‑rate depreciation (costlier imports), sector bottlenecks (e.g., housing, energy), geopolitics or natural disasters, and limited competition/supply constraints can intensify or prolong inflation.

In practice, major inflation episodes usually reflect a mix (e.g., a supply shock plus strong demand, with expectations adjusting upward).

   At its core, **inflation** is a general increase in the prices of goods and services, which results in a decrease in the purchasing power of your money. (In other words, a dollar buys you less today than it did yesterday). 

Economists generally divide the causes of inflation into three main categories, plus a foundational macroeconomic driver. 

Here is a breakdown of what causes inflation:

### 1. Demand-Pull Inflation (Too much money chasing too few goods)
This is the most common cause of inflation. It happens when the demand for goods and services outpaces the economy's ability to produce them. Because consumers are competing for a limited supply of goods, businesses can raise their prices.
* **Causes:** An economic boom, low unemployment, or government stimulus. 
* **Example:** During the post-COVID-19 reopening, consumers had saved up money and received government stimulus checks. When everyone went out to buy cars, electronics, and vacations at the same time, businesses couldn't keep up with the demand, so prices skyrocketed.

### 2. Cost-Push Inflation (The cost of doing business goes up)
This occurs when the cost of producing goods and services rises, and businesses pass those increased costs onto the consumer in the form of higher prices. 
* **Causes:** Spikes in the cost of raw materials (like oil or metals), natural disasters that destroy crops, or supply chain disruptions. 
* **Example:** If a war breaks out in a major oil-producing region, the global supply of oil drops. The price of gas goes up. Because almost all goods are transported by gas-powered trucks, ships, and planes, the cost to deliver *everything* goes up, leading to widespread inflation.

### 3. Built-In Inflation (The Wage-Price Spiral)
This type of inflation is psychological and structural. It relies on people’s **expectations** of the future. 
* **The Cycle:** If workers expect prices to keep rising, they will demand higher wages to maintain their standard of living. Businesses agree to pay higher wages, but to maintain their profit margins, they raise the prices of their products. This causes the cost of living to go up again, prompting workers to ask for another raise. This vicious cycle is known as the **wage-price spiral**.

### 4. Expansion of the Money Supply
Famed economist Milton Friedman once said, *"Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon."* Ultimately, prices are tied to how much money is circulating in the economy.
* **Causes:** When a country’s central bank (like the Federal Reserve in the U.S.) prints too much money or keeps interest rates artificially low for too long, borrowing becomes very cheap. 
* **Effect:** The economy becomes flooded with cash. When the supply of money increases faster than the actual economic output (the creation of real goods and services), the value of the currency drops, meaning prices must go up to compensate. 

---

### Additional Contributing Factors
In the real world, inflation is rarely caused by just one of these factors. It is usually a "perfect storm" of several things happening at once. Other factors include:
* **Devaluation of Currency:** If a country's currency loses value compared to foreign currencies, it makes importing goods from other countries much more expensive.
* **Government Regulations:** New tariffs, taxes, or strict environmental regulations can suddenly increase the cost of producing or importing goods.
* **Corporate Profiteering ("Greedflation"):** Some economists argue that during periods of high inflation, large corporations with market monopolies use the *cover* of inflation to raise prices higher than their actual costs require, expanding their profit margins.

### Is Inflation Always Bad?
Not necessarily. Most central banks actually aim for a low, steady inflation rate of about **2% per year**. A little bit of inflation encourages people to spend and invest their money today (because it will be worth less tomorrow), which keeps the economy growing. 

However, when inflation happens too quickly, wages can't keep up, and people can no longer afford basic necessities.

   ### What Causes Inflation?

Inflation is a sustained rise in the general price level of goods and services in an economy over time, eroding purchasing power. It's typically measured by indices like the Consumer Price Index (CPI) or Producer Price Index (PPI). Economists identify several key causes, often interconnected. Here's a breakdown of the primary drivers, supported by economic theory and real-world examples:

#### 1. **Demand-Pull Inflation** (Too Much Demand Chasing Too Few Goods)
   - **Explanation**: When aggregate demand (consumer spending, investment, government spending, and exports) outpaces supply, sellers raise prices to balance the market.
   - **Key Driver**: Economic booms, low unemployment, or stimulus like tax cuts/fiscal spending.
   - **Evidence/Example**: Post-COVID recovery in 2021–2022, where pent-up demand and government stimulus (e.g., U.S. CARES Act) led to inflation spikes in many countries, with U.S. CPI hitting 9.1% in June 2022.
   - **Theory**: Rooted in Keynesian economics—AD curve shifts rightward.

#### 2. **Cost-Push Inflation** (Rising Production Costs)
   - **Explanation**: Increases in input costs (labor, raw materials, energy) force producers to pass on higher prices to consumers.
   - **Key Drivers**: Supply chain disruptions, wage hikes, commodity price surges (e.g., oil), or import tariffs.
   - **Evidence/Example**: 1970s oil crises (OPEC embargoes) caused global inflation; recent examples include 2022 energy shocks from the Russia-Ukraine war, pushing European inflation above 10%.
   - **Theory**: Shifts the short-run aggregate supply (SRAS) curve leftward.

#### 3. **Built-In (or Wage-Price Spiral) Inflation**
   - **Explanation**: Workers demand higher wages to match rising living costs, prompting businesses to raise prices further, creating a feedback loop.
   - **Key Driver**: Strong labor unions, minimum wage laws, or persistent inflation expectations.
   - **Evidence/Example**: 1980s U.S. under Paul Volcker, where high inflation expectations perpetuated the cycle until aggressive Fed rate hikes broke it.

#### 4. **Monetary Factors** (Excess Money Supply)
   - **Explanation**: Central banks printing too much money (e.g., quantitative easing) increases the money supply faster than economic output, devaluing currency.
   - **Key Driver**: Loose monetary policy, deficit financing via money creation.
   - **Evidence/Example**: Milton Friedman's famous quote: "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." Hyperinflation cases like Zimbabwe (2008, rates >89 sextillion%) or Weimar Germany (1923) from excessive money printing.
   - **Theory**: Quantity Theory of Money (MV = PQ), where M (money supply) grows faster than Q (output).

#### 5. **Other Contributing Factors**
   - **Supply Shocks**: Natural disasters, pandemics, or wars disrupting supply (e.g., 2020–2021 chip shortages inflating car prices).
   - **Expectations**: If people expect inflation, they preemptively raise prices/wages (self-fulfilling prophecy).
   - **Government Policies**: Persistent fiscal deficits funded by borrowing/printing money, or regulated price controls failing.
   - **Global Factors**: Currency depreciation (e.g., Turkey's lira crisis) or commodity booms.

#### Key Nuances
- **Types of Inflation**: Moderate (2–3%, often targeted by central banks like the Fed's 2% goal) vs. hyperinflation (>50% monthly).
- **Measurement Matters**: "Core inflation" excludes food/energy volatility for a clearer picture.
- **Not Always Bad**: Mild inflation encourages spending/investment; deflation (falling prices) can lead to economic stagnation (e.g., Japan's "Lost Decade").
- **Control Mechanisms**: Central banks raise interest rates to curb demand/money supply (e.g., Fed hikes 2022–2023 cooled U.S. inflation from 9% to ~3%).

Inflation is rarely caused by one factor alone—2020s inflation combined demand-pull, cost-push, and monetary expansion.
